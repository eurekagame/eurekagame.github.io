Eureka Quick Reference

Based on the work of the creators of Lancer's Quick Reference Rules, here's all the most important information contained in the sprawling 700-page Eureka rulebook, as of summer 2025. Clinking on any of the in-text hyperlinks will send you to our complete rule compendium with examples and play advice straight from the rulebook.

Moving and Positioning

While many of these sections are abridged versions of Eureka rulebook pages, this section is specifically edited to remove rules we will not be playing with. Specifically, characters in Eureka are not assumed to move at 5 feet per second and rounds are not assumed to be six seconds long. Finally, all of Eureka's combat will occur in Theater of the Mind.

Distance and Nodes

In high-pressure situations, distance will be expressed in nodes. A “node” is basically just a way of marking an area in the space of the game world that can be connected to any number of other areas. When characters are at the same node, they are generally considered to be able to make melee attacks against each other or otherwise directly interact. When using nodes, one Movement can traverse 1 node. A character can move to any adjacent node, as long as nothing is stopping them from doing so.

Nodes are of an indefinite, irregular size and distance from each other. They're not supposed to line up with any uniform real-world measurements, it's just a fast and easy-to-use approximation of character position. In a bar room brawl, there may be one node for each section of the bar counter, one at the entrance, one or two around the seating area, and one or two around the pool table. In a high speed car chase there may be one or two nodes per city block.

Since nodes don't correspond to any real-world distances, determining how far away a gun can shoot or a grenade explosion reaches can be tricky. In small settings, a range of 30 yards might span 15 nodes. In motorized vehicle chases, it might be 1 node - with your opponents 2 nodes away. Your GM will work with you to determine fair ranges when the first gun is brandished.

Speed

Oftentimes, characters will want to traverse more space than two Movements per Round would allow. When this happens, they will need to roll Speed to see how fast they can move relative to other characters. Like Ticks, the Speed stat is ultimately an abstraction - it doesn't directly correlate to any real-world velocity. The purpose of Speed is mostly to determine how fast characters are relative to each other.

Calculating Speed changes the number of Movements that a character can take on their Turn. Instead of two Movements per Turn, a character can take a number of Movements equal to their Speed, but still only one of those Movements may be an Action, as normal. In general, Speed is calculated only once for each character for the duration of an entire Scene, not Round-by-Round. There may be circumstances where Speed ought to change dramatically (such as an on-foot character getting into a car). In that case, it is appropriate to recalculate Speed.

Movement in Eureka cannot be interrupted. This means that if you move to one node to interact with something, like skimming a document, making a melee attack, or picking up a baseball bat, your movement is over, even if your speed has been increased by a speed roll. A character's Speed only needs to be calculated for a given Scene the first time that character tries to move more than the minimum distance they could cover with two Movements. This will mean that some (even most) instances of combat will start and end without anyone ever needing to calculate Speed in the first place.

To calculate Speed:

1. Roll 1D6+[Skill]+[Acceleration], with a minimum of 1

2. Divide the result by 2 (rounding up).

3. Add +1

The result is the character's Speed. If a character is on-foot or using a manually-powered vehicle such as a skateboard or bicycle, use Athletics as the Skill. If they're using a motor vehicle, use Driving (or another relevant Skill, like Boating).
