Eureka Quick Reference

Based on the work of the creators of Lancer's Quick Reference Rules, here's all the most important information contained in the sprawling 700-page Eureka rulebook, as of summer 2025. Clinking on any of the in-text hyperlinks will send you to our complete rule compendium with examples and play advice straight from the rulebook.

Character Health

Hit Points (HP) represent a character's physical condition. Characters have two separate types of HP: Superficial and Penetrative.

Superficial HP is affected by damage on the surface like bruises, burns, and shallow cuts. Fists, fire, and just about anything that isn't immediately life-threatening cause Superficial Damage.Guns, knives, baseball bats, and serious car crashes cause Penetrative Damage.

Penetrative HP is affected by damage that breaks bones, punctures organs, and rends flesh.

Any Superficial Damage past 0 Superficial HP “spills over” into Penetrative HP. Any character reduced to 0 Penetrative HP dies immediately.

The Injury Roll

When a character is reduced to 1 Superficial HP, when reduced to 0 Superficial HP, and any time they take any kind of damage while at 0 Superficial HP, they must make an Athletics roll to determine the severity of their injury

Full Success: The character does not gain a status, though of course having low HP is a problem of its own.

Partial Success: The character gains the Superficial Injury status. Then, roll 1d6. If the die lands on a 6, the character recieves a Grievous Wound.

Failure: The character gains the Superficial Incapacitation status. Then, roll 1d6. If the die lands on 6, the character receives a Grievous Wound. If the die lands on 1, the character falls unconscious.

When a character is reduced to 1 Penetrative HP, they also must make an Athletics/Injury roll. Statuses are Penetrative instead of Superficial, and any Grievous Wounds are permanent instead of temporary.

An Investigator's character sheet has four boxes, one for each status (Superficial Injury, Superficial Incapacitation, Penetrative Injury, and Penetrative Incapacitation) These statuses can stack. An investigator can hypothetically have all four at once. Sounds like a bad day!

A character with the Injury status takes a -2 penalty to all rolls (except Wealth rolls).

A character with the Incapacitation status (of either type) will be capable of at most 1 Speed and will make all rolls with 1d6 instead of 2d6. A 1 is still considered a Failure regardless of modifiers, but a 6 is not an automatic Full Success.

The Injury and Incapacitation statuses are removed when the matching type of HP is fully restored.

If a character has both the Superficial and Penetrative Incapacitation statuses, they are treated as being unconscious.

Unconscious characters cannot act. Unconciousness lasts until the end of the scene or for 1 Tick depending on the circumstances. Characters are likely in fiction not unconcious for minutes at a time - the status can represent shock, trauma, delirium, or other reasons why your investigator might be non-functional.

Grevious Wounds

A Grievous Wound is any kind of disabling wound that would have a specific lasting effect on a part of the body. When a character is injured and takes a Grevious Wound, the Narrator decides what kind of Grievous Wound a character takes, based on what kind of weapon is doing the damage, where it hit, and what they think would be most interesting for the situation at hand

If the status that caused the Grievous Wound is Superficial, then the Grievous Wound is temporary. The effects cease when the status is removed. However, if the status that caused the Grievous Wound is Penetrative, then the Grievous Wound is permanent. The character has to live with it forever. If a body part that was already currently suffering a temporary Grievous Wound takes a second Grievous Wound, that Grievous Wound becomes permanent.

Depending on the time period or tech level of the campaign, a character may be able to get a prosthetic or physical therapy to mitigate the effects of a permanent Grievous Wound, though this will usually have to happen in between adventures.

Health

Under normal circumstances, characters recover 1 point of Superficial HP the morning after a night of rest, but Penetrative HP must be restored by either a Medicine roll or long-term medical care

The Medicine Roll

Different types of HP are tracked separately: a character must declare what they are restoring before they roll. Characters may only make a Medicine Roll on one given investigator per scene. Once an investigator has regained a given type of HP from a Medicine roll, they must lose a point of that same HP before another Medicine roll can be made to restore that type of HP.

Full Success: 2 HP restored.

Partial Success: 1 HP restored.

Failure: No HP restored.

Characters may only make this type of Medicine roll on a given investigator once per Scene, per type of HP. Once an investigator has regained a given type of HP from a Medicine roll, they must lose a point of that same HP before another Medicine roll can be made to restore that type of HP.

Medical Facilities & Long-Term Care

When a character visits a medical facility, they can receive immediate care, long-term care, or both. Medical facilities fall into three categories: hospitals, clinics, and “back-alley.”

Hospital: Full-service facilities. +4 Medicine.[1] Can provide immediate and long-term care. No bonus or penalty to the Wealth roll (see below). Will report gunshots, stab wounds, etc. to the authorities

Clinic: Outpatient and minor emergency facilities. +3 Medicine. Can only provide immediate care. +2 Contextual bonus to the Wealth roll. May report gunshots, stab wounds, etc. to the authorities.

'Back-Alley': Unregistered, unlicensed, illegal, or otherwise “under-the-table” facilities. +3 Medicine. Can provide immediate and long-term care. No bonus or penalty to the Wealth roll. Will not report anything to the authorities.

Even finding a “back-alley” facility can be tricky, they don't exactly advertise. A character attempting to find one will have to ask dodgy questions or make a Full Success on a reversed Wealth roll, unless there is some other obvious reason the character would have access to one.

The Wealth Roll

Regardless of the type of care, it's not free. An investigator who needs to pay for medical care will need to make a Wealth roll to afford it, before benefitting from either form of treatment.

Full Success: They can afford medical treatment without issue.

Partial Success: They can afford medical treatment, but it's a hit to the wallet, and they must make a Poverty Composure roll.

Failure: They must take on medical debt to afford treatment. They must make a Poverty Composure roll, and take a -2 penalty to Wealth for the remainder of the adventure.

After the roll is made, a character may decline medical treatment, choosing to forgo the Composure roll and any medical debt

For immediate medical care, the Narrator makes a Medicine roll for the facility. This works exactly like a Medicine roll made by another character, following all of the same guidelines. For long-term care, a character has a chance to restore HP (of both types) every day.
