Eureka Quick Reference

Based on the work of the creators of Lancer's Quick Reference Rules, here's all the most important information contained in the sprawling 700-page Eureka rulebook, as of summer 2025. Clinking on any of the in-text hyperlinks will send you to our complete rule compendium with examples and play advice straight from the rulebook.

Basic Rolls

To make a check in Eureka, add the results of rolling 2d6 and the relevant modifier. The success of the roll is determined as follows:

Full Success: A 10+ on the roll. The character achieves the intended result of their roll without negative consequences.

Partial Success: 7-9 on the roll. The character achieves their intended results but with consequences or less than intended effect.

Failure: 6 or lower. The attempt fails.

In the event of partial successes and failures, depending on the context, consequences may in the fiction be portrayed as a result of bad luck or a failure of character skill. If a player's character is an expert hacker, it might feel better to hear their investigator failed a tech check due to a power outage than a coding bug. Players are encouraged to contribute to the GM's ultimate ruling on both the mechanical and narrative effects of partial successes and failures.

Modifiers

There are two types of positive modifiers in Eureka: base and contextual.

Base modifiers represent an investigator's natural skill and comes from skills or traits on the investigator's character sheet. The sum of all Base modifiers and penalties on a given roll cannot exceed an investigator's current Composure.

Contextual modifiers apply in certain contexts as defined by the rulebook, like shooting a gun from a long distance or making an investigation check while wounded.

Your character may also have negative modifiers, called penalties, which are reflected in skills and traits and are subtracted from base modifiers before composure is considered.

For an individual roll, the result is always calculated with the following method:

1. Roll 2d6 and compute the sum.

2. Evaluate the sum of only base and negative modifiers. Compare this value to the Composure skill. If higher, reduce the value to the investigator's Composure score. Only now is the result of this step added to the sum from step 1.

3. Add any contextual modifiers to the roll for your final result

Assisting with Rolls

Characters can help one another with some rolls in some circumstances. Assists should almost never be used for Composure, Attack, Speed, and Injury rolls. They should rarely be used for Investigation rolls. An assist roll is reserved for situations where a character can

Full Success: +2 Contextual modifier to the Main Roll.

Partial Success: +1 Contextual modifier to the Main Roll.

Failure: -2 penalty to the main roll.

More than one investigator can roll to assist with a Main Roll, but the GM can use discretion when determining if additional assists would help.

In the event an investigator can assist with an Investigation roll, the assisting character also earns Investigation Points.
